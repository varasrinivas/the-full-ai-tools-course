## TL;DR

The endpoint is a classic N+1: `ClaimHistoryService.historyFor` claims to do "ONE query" but actually issues **one claim query per distinct patient in the queue**, and each of those queries is a **full scan of the unindexed `claim` table**. On the shipped seed data that's **93 SQL statements per call scanning ~40,000 rows to return ~360**. It was introduced by commit `674272e` ("Simplify claim history lookup with a per-patient map"), which quietly replaced the real batch query with a loop.

## How the cost scales

Let **Q** = queue rows, **P** = distinct patients in the queue (P ≤ Q), **C** = total rows in `claim`:

- **SQL round trips per call: 1 + P** (previously a constant 2). The loop is sequential, so latency grows linearly in P from round-trip overhead alone — this is the growth users feel on a networked Postgres.
- **Rows scanned per call: ≈ P × C**, because each of the P queries seq-scans all C claim rows (no index) and sorts its matches. Since the claim table grows with the patient population, doubling the business (queue and claim history both 2×) roughly **quadruples** the database work — effectively O(P·C), superlinear in queue size, vs. the old O(C) single scan.
